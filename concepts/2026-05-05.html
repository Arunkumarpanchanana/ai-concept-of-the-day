Foundations INTERMEDIATE

In-context learning is a large language model's ability to adapt its behavior and generate desired outputs based solely on the examples and instructions provided within the input prompt, without any updates to its internal parameters.

In-context learning describes how powerful pre-trained models, especially large language models, can perform new tasks or adapt their output style simply by observing examples and instructions within the input prompt itself. This is distinct from traditional machine learning where a model's parameters, or weights, are updated through training on a dataset. Instead, during in-context learning, the model leverages its vast pre-trained knowledge to recognize patterns, infer rules, and apply them to new inputs presented in the same prompt. It's like giving a highly knowledgeable expert a few examples of a task and asking them to continue, rather than teaching them from scratch. The model conditions its generation on the entire prompt, treating the examples as demonstrations of the desired input-output mapping for the current inference pass.

Why this matters

In-context learning is fundamental for prompt engineering and unlocks immense flexibility in how large language models are used. It allows practitioners to rapidly adapt a single model to countless specific tasks—from text summarization to code generation—without needing to fine-tune or retrain it. This significantly reduces development time and computational costs, making models highly versatile and accessible for a wide range of applications, enabling few-shot learning where only a handful of examples are sufficient.

Concrete example

Consider a large language model like gpt-3.5 being used for sentiment classification. Instead of fine-tuning it, you can provide in-context examples in the prompt. For instance: "Classify the sentiment of the following reviews as 'Positive', 'Negative', or 'Neutral'. Example 1: 'The movie was fantastic!' -> Positive. Example 2: 'I found the plot quite dull.' -> Negative. Example 3: 'It was an average experience.' -> Neutral. Now, classify: 'The customer service was surprisingly helpful.' ->". The model, using its in-context learning ability, will likely output "Positive" by recognizing the pattern established by the preceding examples and applying it to the new review.

Common misconception

A common misconception is that in-context learning involves the model actually 'learning' in the sense of updating its internal weights or parameters. In reality, in-context learning does not modify the model's underlying architecture or weights; it is an emergent capability of large models to condition their output based on the prompt during a single forward pass.
